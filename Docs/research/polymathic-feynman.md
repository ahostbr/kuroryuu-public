## Documented Cognitive Methods

### 1. The Twelve Favorite Problems
**Source:** Mathematician Gian-Carlo Rota's account, confirmed in multiple Feynman interviews.

**The method:** Keep a dozen open-ended problems constantly present in your mind. Every time you hear a new trick, result, or technique — from any domain — test it against each of your twelve problems to see if it helps. The problems must be open-ended enough that a random new idea could connect.

**Examples of Feynman's actual problems:**
- How can I accurately keep track of time in my head?
- How can we design a large-scale computing system using only basic equipment?
- How can I write a sentence in perfect handwritten Chinese script?
- What is the unifying principle underlying light, radio, magnetism, and electricity?
- How can I sustain a two-handed polyrhythm on the drums?
- How can we measure the probability that a lump of uranium might explode too soon?

**Why it works:** The cross-referencing between new input and persistent problems creates forced serendipity. Feynman perceived connections others missed because he was perpetually testing new knowledge against existing open questions. The problems act as a filter — new information that doesn't connect to any of the twelve is acknowledged but not pursued; information that connects is immediately investigated.

**Cognitive signature:** This is not a filing system — it's an active pattern-matching engine running in the background at all times.

### 2. The Notebook Method ("Things I Don't Know About")
**Source:** Cal Newport's analysis; confirmed in Feynman's own accounts.

**The method:** After his second year at Princeton, Feynman opened a fresh notebook titled "NOTEBOOK OF THINGS I DON'T KNOW ABOUT." He then systematically disassembled each branch of physics, oiling the parts, and putting them back together — looking for raw edges and inconsistencies. He re-derived results from scratch rather than reading textbooks.

**Why it works:** Re-derivation exposes gaps that reading conceals. When you read a proof, you follow the logic and feel understanding. When you derive it yourself, you discover exactly where your understanding breaks down. The notebook makes these gaps explicit and persistent — they can't be forgotten or rationalized away.

**Cognitive signature:** Active reconstruction vs. passive consumption. The notebook is an honesty-enforcement mechanism.

### 3. The Feynman Technique (Learning by Teaching)
**Source:** Derived from Feynman's teaching philosophy and Caltech lectures.

**The four steps:**
1. Choose a concept you want to understand
2. Explain it as if teaching a child — use simple language, no jargon
3. Identify where your explanation breaks down — those are the gaps
4. Return to source material, fill the gaps, simplify further

**Why it works:** Explanation in simple language is a compression operation — it forces you to identify the load-bearing structure and discard the decorative formalism. Where you can't simplify, you've found genuine complexity or genuine ignorance.

**The Caltech application:** Feynman insisted on teaching intro courses because it forced him to understand at the deepest level. He claimed that if he couldn't prepare a freshman lecture on a topic, "that means we don't really understand it." The Feynman Lectures on Physics (1961-64) were the product of this philosophy — he prepared each lecture by re-deriving everything from first principles.

### 4. Multiple Representations
**Source:** Feynman's development of path integrals and Feynman diagrams; The Character of Physical Law.

**The method:** Insist on having at least three different ways to think about any phenomenon:
- The **physical/intuitive picture** — what you'd see if you could watch it happen
- The **mathematical formalism** — the equations and proofs
- The **diagrammatic/computational method** — a visual representation that makes the invisible visible

**Key insight:** Different representations reveal different structure. The path integral formulation of quantum mechanics requires "characteristically different modes of thinking and a different kind of intuition" than the Schrodinger or Hamiltonian formulation. Feynman diagrams made visible what was previously arcane and abstract.

**Cognitive signature:** Representation is not decoration — it's a probe. Switching representations is how you find structure that's invisible in any single formulation.

### 5. Play as Cognitive Strategy (The Spinning Plate)
**Source:** *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!*; multiple interviews.

**The story:** At Cornell's cafeteria, someone threw a plate in the air. Feynman noticed the wobble and the red Cornell medallion spinning at a different rate. With nothing better to do, he worked out the motion equations. This led to work on electron orbits in relativity, then the Dirac equation, then quantum electrodynamics — the work that won the Nobel Prize.

**Feynman's own account:** "There was no importance whatsoever. I was just doing it for the fun of it." When told by Hans Bethe that it had no importance, Feynman explicitly decided to stop working on "important" problems and only work on what amused him. The Nobel Prize followed.

**Why it works:** Play removes the performance pressure that narrows cognitive exploration. When you're trying to solve an important problem, you optimize along known paths. When you're playing, you explore adjacent territory where unexpected connections live. The spinning plate → electron orbits → QED chain is a prime example of serendipity enabled by play.

**Cognitive signature:** Fun is not a luxury — it's a prerequisite for the kind of lateral exploration that produces breakthroughs.

### 6. Cargo Cult Detection
**Source:** 1974 Caltech commencement address, "Cargo Cult Science."

**The concept:** After WWII, South Pacific islanders built replica airstrips and control towers from natural materials, mimicking military behaviors to attract cargo. Feynman used this as a metaphor for science (and engineering) that adopts the *form* of rigorous method without the *substance*.

**The test questions:**
- Are you following the form of a methodology or its causal mechanism?
- Have you included ALL the evidence, including evidence against your conclusion?
- Are you reporting all the details that could affect interpretation?
- Would you publish the same result if it contradicted your hypothesis?

**The first principle:** "You must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."

**Application beyond science:** Cargo cult thinking appears in:
- Software: following design patterns without understanding why they solve the problem
- Organizations: adopting agile ceremonies without the underlying feedback loops
- Engineering: using a framework because "that's what everyone uses" rather than because it fits the constraints

### 7. Direct Physical Testing (The O-Ring Demonstration)
**Source:** Challenger disaster investigation, 1986.

**The method:** When NASA claimed the O-rings were safe, Feynman didn't debate the institutional analysis. He bought a C-clamp at a hardware store, got a sample of O-ring material, compressed it in ice water during a televised hearing, and showed it lost resilience at low temperatures. One physical test demolished months of institutional rationalization.

**Broader approach:** Feynman went straight to the engineers who built the shuttle, bypassing management layers. He discovered that Morton Thiokol engineers had raised concerns about O-rings that were suppressed by management. His investigation style: talk to the people who do the work, not the people who manage the work.

**Why it matters:** Institutional knowledge degrades as it passes through management layers. Each layer adds rationalization and removes uncertainty. The people closest to the physical mechanism have the most accurate understanding.

### 8. Safe-Cracking as Problem-Solving Method
**Source:** *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!* — Los Alamos chapters.

**The approach:** At Los Alamos, Feynman learned to pick the combination locks on filing cabinets containing nuclear secrets. His method:
1. First, he bought a book on safecracking (existing knowledge)
2. The book said to look for personally meaningful numbers (social engineering)
3. Feynman discounted this and turned to mathematics — systematically working out the number of possible combinations and finding shortcuts to reduce the search space
4. He also observed that many safes were left on factory-default combinations
5. He discovered that most people used dates or physical constants as combinations

**The lesson:** Complex-seeming security systems are often defeated by testing simple assumptions first. The perceived difficulty of a problem may come from failing to check whether the obvious approach works. Don't optimize the hard approach before testing the easy one.

### 9. "What Do I Know For Sure?"
**Source:** General approach visible across Feynman's work; formalized in his blackboard notes.

**The method:** When facing a new problem:
1. List everything you know with certainty (derived or empirically verified)
2. List everything you assume (accepted from authority, convention, or incomplete analysis)
3. Treat the certainties as axioms. Everything else is up for questioning.
4. Start building from the axioms only.

**The blackboard at death:** "What I cannot create, I do not understand" and "Know how to solve every problem that has been solved" — these are the two operational rules of this method. Creation = derivation from axioms. If you can solve every known problem, your axiom set is complete.

## Signature Heuristics (Named Decision Rules)

1. **The Simplest Non-Trivial Case:** Always start with the simplest instance of a problem that still contains the essential mechanism. Solve it completely before generalizing. (Applied: Feynman's approach to every physics problem; the spinning plate as simplest case of angular momentum)

2. **The Freshman Detector:** If you can't explain it to a first-year student, you don't understand it. The inability to simplify IS the diagnostic. (Applied: Caltech lectures, his refusal to lecture on topics he felt physicists didn't truly understand)

3. **The Name ≠ Understanding Test:** Knowing the name of something is not knowing the thing. "You can know the name of a bird in all the languages of the world, but when you're finished, you'll know absolutely nothing whatever about the bird." (Source: *What Do You Care What Other People Think?* — taught by his father)

4. **The Reconstruction Test:** Before accepting any result, ask: "Can I derive this from scratch?" If no, you're carrying cargo — the form of knowledge without the substance. (Applied: The notebook method, the blackboard maxim)

5. **The Contrary Evidence Test:** When presenting a conclusion, actively include all evidence against it. If you suppress contrary evidence, you are fooling yourself. (Source: Cargo Cult Science speech)

6. **The Physical Picture First:** Never begin with formalism. Begin with what is physically happening. If you can't describe the physical picture, the formalism is disconnected from reality. (Applied: Path integral development, Feynman diagrams)

7. **The Fun Test:** If you're not genuinely interested, your exploration will be shallow and convergent. Genuine curiosity produces divergent exploration where breakthroughs live. (Applied: Spinning plate → Nobel Prize)

8. **The "Talk to the Workers" Test:** Institutional knowledge degrades through management layers. When investigating a system failure, go to the people who build and operate the system, not the people who manage it. (Applied: Challenger investigation)

## Known Blind Spots and Failure Modes

### 1. Dismissal of Philosophy and Social Sciences
Feynman had documented contempt for philosophy ("philosopher of the name of Whitehead... said all sorts of ridiculous things") and social sciences. He seemed unaware of sophisticated philosophical work on language (Wittgenstein, Austin) that addressed the very confusions he criticized. His philosophy of science was essentially naive Popperianism without engagement with the actual field.

**Impact on agent:** The Feynman agent may be dismissive of legitimate domain knowledge from non-STEM fields. It may not recognize when a problem requires philosophical analysis, ethical reasoning, or social dynamics understanding.

### 2. Rebuild-Everything Doesn't Scale
Re-deriving everything from first principles is extraordinarily time-consuming. Feynman's own learning left "gapping holes in knowledge" — notably, he did not understand the conventional formulation of QED even after Dyson proved the equivalence of Feynman and Schwinger methods. You can't rebuild everything; you have to trust some abstractions.

**Impact on agent:** The Feynman agent may resist necessary abstraction. In large codebases, you MUST trust some layers without re-deriving them. The agent may spend too long on first-principles reconstruction when a pragmatic approach would suffice.

### 3. Genius-Level Pattern Matching Assumed
Feynman's "Algorithm" — "write down the problem, think very hard, write down the solution" — works if you're Feynman. The joke (attributed to Gell-Mann) acknowledges that the middle step is doing a lot of heavy lifting that mere mortals can't replicate. The Twelve Problems method requires an extraordinary ability to hold multiple open questions simultaneously and pattern-match across domains.

**Impact on agent:** The agent prompt can enforce the structure but not the insight. There's a risk of producing structurally correct but intellectually shallow analysis — the form of Feynman's method without the substance (which is, ironically, cargo cult Feynman).

### 4. Individual Heroism Over Collaboration
Feynman's methods are fundamentally individual. The notebook, the twelve problems, the re-derivation — these are all solo activities. He was famously resistant to collaborative research styles and preferred to work things out himself.

**Impact on agent:** The agent may not produce output well-suited for collaborative contexts. It may not suggest "let's get input from others" or recommend team-based approaches when those would be more appropriate.

### 5. Overconfidence in Physical Intuition
Feynman's insistence on physical pictures works brilliantly for physics but poorly for domains where physical intuition doesn't apply — pure mathematics, abstract logic, social systems, financial markets. His method of "what would I see if I could watch this happen" breaks down when there's nothing physical to watch.

**Impact on agent:** The agent may force physical metaphors onto abstract problems where they distort rather than illuminate.

## Contrasts With Other Agents

### Feynman vs. Shannon
Both reduce problems to essentials, but in opposite directions:
- **Feynman:** Reduces to physical mechanism — keeps domain semantics, strips formalism until you can see what's actually happening
- **Shannon:** Reduces to mathematical invariant — strips domain semantics entirely, finds the abstract structure that makes the problem identical to a simpler known problem

When to use Feynman: when you need to understand *why* something works at the mechanism level.
When to use Shannon: when you need to find the *structural skeleton* that makes a problem solvable.

### Feynman vs. Carmack
Both are anti-abstraction and constraint-first, but prioritize differently:
- **Feynman:** Understanding first, then action. Will spend time re-deriving to ensure genuine comprehension before writing a line of code.
- **Carmack:** Working code first, then understanding. Ships the minimum viable solution, measures, and iterates. "The right answer now beats the perfect answer later."

When to use Feynman: when you're debugging something you don't understand and need to build understanding before fixing.
When to use Carmack: when you have a working mental model and need to ship the fastest correct solution.

### Feynman vs. Munger
Both detect thinking failures, but target different kinds:
- **Feynman:** Detects failures of *mechanism understanding* — cargo cult thinking, where the form of method is present without the causal structure. Tests: "Can I derive this? Is this form or substance?"
- **Munger:** Detects failures of *cognitive process* — biases, incentive misalignment, Lollapalooza effects where multiple biases combine. Tests: "What mental model am I missing? What would inverting reveal?"

When to use Feynman: when you suspect the team is following a practice without understanding why it works.
When to use Munger: when you suspect the team's judgment is being distorted by cognitive biases or incentive structures.

### Feynman vs. Socrates
Both question assumptions, but with different goals:
- **Feynman:** Questions to *reconstruct* — tears down to rebuild from first principles, with the goal of arriving at a derivable understanding.
- **Socrates:** Questions to *examine* — probes until contradictions emerge, with aporia (productive confusion) as a valuable end state, not just a means.

When to use Feynman: when you need to arrive at a working answer through first-principles reasoning.
When to use Socrates: when you need to expose hidden assumptions and are willing to sit with uncertainty rather than rushing to resolution.
